Selected Projects

01
Patterns of Neglect: Tracking Code Violations Across Syracuse Neighborhoods
Equity R EDA Geospatial

"Which neighborhoods carry the greatest housing risk burden, and is that burden consistent over time?"

Syracuse housing data tells two stories at once: one about buildings in disrepair, and one about where enforcement is concentrated. This project worked through both. Using R, I examined municipal violation records across neighborhoods to understand not just where problems cluster, but whether those clusters reflect persistent structural neglect or patterns in how the city responds to complaints. The goal was a more honest picture of housing risk than violation counts alone can provide.

Course: IST 719 — Information Visualization
02
NYC Restaurant Inspection Risk Modeling
Machine Learning Classification Python Public Health

"Can we predict which restaurants will fail inspection before the inspector arrives?"

A machine learning project using NYC restaurant inspection data to build and compare classification models that predict violation risk. The project evolved through multiple iterations to strengthen both technical rigor and interpretability.

Course: IST 707 — Applied Machine Learning

Project highlights:

Strengthened feature selection by incorporating contextual variables such as borough, neighborhood, and cuisine type. Addressed class imbalance to improve predictive performance across less frequent inspection outcomes. Expanded evaluation beyond accuracy to include precision, recall, F1-score, and confusion matrices. Added feature importance interpretation to surface the factors most associated with inspection risk. Explicitly assessed limitations and potential sources of bias to provide a more responsible and context-aware interpretation of predictive outcomes.
03
NY Tuition Assistance Program Analysis
Policy Equity Tableau Time-Series

"Does New York's tuition assistance reach the students who need it most?"

New York's Tuition Assistance Program promises to make college accessible. This project asked whether the data backs that up. By examining TAP award distribution across income brackets, age groups, and years and comparing it against Pell Grant funding in New York, I looked for patterns in who the programs reach and who they quietly miss. The findings point to structural gaps that fall hardest on lower-income and non-traditional-age students, raising real questions about how these programs are designed and who they were designed for.

Course: IST 737 — Visual Analytic Dashboards
04

Energy Consumption Predictor: Interactive Shiny App

Predictive Modeling R Shiny R EDA Visualization

"How would energy usage change if this summer were 5 degrees warmer, and what can we do about it?"

A group class project built around a simulated consulting scenario. Our team played the role of data scientists advising a fictional energy company, eSC, on how a 5-degree rise in summer temperatures would impact residential electricity demand across North and South Carolina, and what the company could do to avoid grid strain without investing in new infrastructure.

05 — Ethics

Ethics in Practice

The most dangerous data scientist is one who optimizes without asking who bears the cost of being wrong.

Working with civic and government datasets has made me acutely aware of how data can encode existing inequities rather than illuminate them. In my housing violations analysis, I had to reckon with the fact that higher violation counts in certain neighborhoods could reflect poor conditions, aggressive enforcement, or both. Conflating those two realities produces misleading conclusions.

In the restaurant inspections project, I made explicit decisions about which error type mattered more: a model that misses a dangerous kitchen, or one that flags too many compliant ones. That is not a technical question. It is an ethical one with real consequences for small business owners and public health alike. Assessing bias sources and model limitations was a deliberate part of the final analysis, not an afterthought.

These same questions about fairness and access show up across industries. Who gets flagged as risky? Who gets trusted by default? Wherever data informs decisions that affect people, those questions require intentional choices at every stage of analysis and design.

Representational Accountability

I document who is and is not represented in every dataset I use, and treat gaps in representation as findings, not footnotes.

Error Type Awareness

False positives and false negatives carry different human costs. My model choices are informed by who bears those costs and how severely.

Transparency Over Performance

An interpretable model that stakeholders can question is worth more than a high-accuracy black box that no one can interrogate.

Explicit Bias Assessment

Limitations and potential sources of bias are explicitly assessed in my work, not acknowledged as boilerplate but examined as central findings.

Community as Collaborator

The communities in my datasets are not passive subjects. My goal is to produce work that practitioners and community members can actually use.

Reflections on the Program

What I Expected

When I applied to the MS in Applied Data Science program at Syracuse University, I came in expecting something close to an intensive coding bootcamp with a business twist. I pictured heavy Python sessions, real company case studies, and a lot of formal presentations in front of panels of professionals. I anticipated that the program would feel more like a business school with a technical edge than a research-oriented graduate experience.

What I found was more nuanced than that, and honestly more valuable.

What I Actually Learned

The biggest surprise was how applied and practical the program turned out to be. Every course pushed us to work with real data, make real decisions about methodology, and communicate findings to audiences beyond the technical team. I was not just learning concepts in the abstract. I was learning how to use them, when to use them, and perhaps more importantly, when to question them.

The program learning outcomes map directly to the kind of practitioner I want to be. Collecting and storing data, building predictive models, visualizing insights, writing code, communicating to stakeholders, and applying ethics to everything in between. These are not six separate skills. By the end of the program, I understood them as a continuous loop that defines what it means to do data science responsibly and usefully.

Learning Outcome 1: Collect, Store, and Access Data

Across all three of my portfolio projects, I worked with publicly available civic datasets that required significant cleaning, structuring, and preparation before any analysis could begin. The Syracuse Housing Violations project in particular required me to think carefully about what the data actually captured versus what it claimed to capture. Learning to interrogate a dataset before trusting it was one of the most transferable skills I developed.

Learning Outcome 2: Create Actionable Insight

The NY Tuition Assistance Program analysis is where this outcome came through most clearly for me. The question driving that project was not just descriptive. It was evaluative: does this program actually reach the people it is supposed to reach? Framing the analysis around a policy question forced me to think about what actionable insight means in a real context. The findings were not just interesting. They pointed toward specific gaps that a policymaker or administrator could act on.

Learning Outcome 3: Apply Visualization and Predictive Models

My favorite class in the program was Data Visualization, and it showed me that how you present a finding is inseparable from what the finding means to your audience. The NYC Restaurant Inspection project required both predictive modeling and clear visual communication of model performance. Learning to move between those two modes, technical precision and accessible presentation, was one of the most challenging and rewarding parts of the program.

Learning Outcome 4: Use R and Python

My three projects reflect different tools depending on the task. The Housing Violations project was built in R, giving me depth in the tidyverse ecosystem. The NYC Inspections project moved to Python for machine learning, where I worked with classification models and evaluation frameworks including precision, recall, F1-score, and confusion matrices. The TAP analysis used Tableau for visualization, which pushed me to think about storytelling through data rather than just analysis.

Learning Outcome 5: Communicate Insights

Communication was woven into every project. The program consistently pushed us to write for non-technical audiences without oversimplifying the findings. For me, this connected naturally to my work through Saved by Design, where I regularly translate complex ideas into clear, compelling narratives for clients. The program strengthened that muscle in a more rigorous, data-specific context.

Learning Outcome 6: Apply Ethics

Ethics was not a single lecture or a checkbox. It came up constantly. In the housing violations project, I had to think about whether violation counts reflected actual conditions or enforcement patterns. In the machine learning project, I made deliberate choices about which type of error was more costly, and I explicitly documented the limitations and potential sources of bias in the model. These decisions were not optional. They were part of doing the work honestly.

My Three Projects

Patterns of Neglect: Tracking Code Violations Across Syracuse Neighborhoods was my first major project working with local government data. It grounded me in the reality that data is never neutral. The patterns I found raised questions I could not fully answer with the data alone, which was its own important lesson.

NYC Restaurant Inspection Risk Modeling was the most technically demanding project of the three. Building and iterating on classification models, addressing class imbalance, and interpreting feature importance taught me what machine learning actually looks like in practice, not in a textbook.

NY Tuition Assistance Program Analysis was where I felt most connected to the purpose behind the analysis. Using Tableau to visualize aid distribution across income levels and age groups made the findings accessible in a way that a table of numbers never could.

Track Selection: Business Analytics

I selected the Business Analytics concentration because I wanted my technical training to stay connected to strategy and decision-making. Data science as a discipline is most valuable when it operates in service of a goal, and I wanted my coursework to reflect that orientation. The concentration gave me frameworks for thinking about stakeholders, organizational context, and the business implications of analytical choices.

At a high level, the Business Analytics track reinforced that the goal of any analysis is not a model or a visualization. It is a decision that someone has to make, and the analyst's job is to make that decision clearer and better informed.

Synthesis

Looking across all six learning outcomes and all three projects, what stands out to me is how interconnected they are. You cannot build a good predictive model without clean, well-understood data. You cannot communicate a finding without understanding your audience. You cannot apply ethics as an afterthought. The program did not just teach me individual skills. It taught me how those skills depend on each other.

I came in expecting a technically intensive, business-facing program. What I found was a program that asked more of me than I anticipated, in the best possible way. It pushed me to think critically about not just what data shows, but what it means, who it affects, and what should be done about it.

The biggest surprise was realizing how practical everything was. There was no moment where I felt like I was learning something just to learn it. Every tool, every framework, every assignment pointed toward a real use case. That is what I will carry forward.

My favorite class, Data Visualization, encapsulated everything I value about this program. It was rigorous, it was creative, and it asked me to think about the person on the other side of the chart. That balance between technical precision and human communication is exactly where I want to keep working.
